The Copyright Design and Patents Act 1988

The Copyright Design and Patents Act 1988 has been put in place in order to protect the ownership of the original creator of a peace of digital work or media. Anything that you have created under this act is your copyright and so gives you full control over distribution. This means that a creator is able to prevent people ripping off their work or sharing it arround with a group of friends without breaking the law.

The The Copyright Design and Patents Act disallows people from sharing a peice of copyrighted work with a friend, selling or renting the work (without permision) or using the work on a network unless it is allowed in the licence. An important thing to remember is that when you buy a peice of software you don't own the software itself, instead you own a licence to be able use it.
Some copyright holders will go out of their way to prevent any of these things from happening by using specialised software that checks the work is only on allowed computers.

Some ways companies and copyright holders prevent software piracy:

  • An agreement is come to between the developer and the user that must be agreed to before the software can be used called a license agreement. Any breach of this agreement is illegal.
  • Some software will require the use of a unique license key to activate or use.
  • Some software will require the existance of a hard CD or DVD to run
  • Some Software will require registration
